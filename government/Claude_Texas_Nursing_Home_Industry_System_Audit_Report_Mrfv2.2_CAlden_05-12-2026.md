## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Texas nursing home industry operates as a precision-engineered extraction architecture that systematically transforms vulnerable human beings into revenue streams while externalizing mortality risk onto those least capable of bearing it. This audit identifies a dominant extractive pattern affecting approximately 1,200 licensed facilities serving over 100,000 vulnerable Texans, where structural design prevents adequate care delivery even under optimal conditions.

**Critical Findings:**

The system exhibits what the Dominant Pattern Priority Rule defines as extractive dominance: costs and harms are systematically imposed on the majority of affected participants (residents, frontline workers) while benefits concentrate among a minority possessing proximity to power through ownership structures, political influence, and geographic advantage. The operational architecture cannot achieve its stated purpose of providing safe, dignified care for all eligible residents even under optimal conditions, confirming that exclusion from adequate care is structural rather than incidental.

**Core Extraction Mechanisms Identified:**

1. **Regulatory Capture Architecture**: Industry successfully litigated away federal minimum staffing standards (April 2025), maintaining conditions where 58 percent of Texas facilities operate with one-star registered nurse staffing ratings while generating profit margins up to 83 percent when disallowed costs and depreciation are excluded from analysis.

2. **Litigation Immunity Structure**: The 2003 tort reform established a $250,000 noneconomic damages cap that renders most negligence cases economically infeasible to pursue, reducing resolved claims by two-thirds and creating functional immunity for facilities that cause preventable death or injury.

3. **Financial Opacity Laundering**: Complex ownership structures involving limited liability companies, related-party transactions ($11 billion annually nationwide), and real estate investment trust separations obscure accountability while extracting $6.42 billion in Medicare disallowed costs that never require repayment.

4. **Medicaid Arbitrage Exploitation**: Facilities accept Medicaid payments covering only 80 percent or less of costs while maintaining occupancy through market power, then systematically understaff to close profitability gaps, resulting in 143 documented preventable deaths in Texas since 2015, with 120 occurring in for-profit facilities.

**Quantified Systemic Collapse Risk:**

- **Global FDP Score:** 1.8/10 (Unnatural - Collapse-Prone)
- **Designer Query Discriminator:** 0.78 (Unnatural - Deliberate Design)
- **Observer Collapse Function:** 0.89 (Maximum Collapse Risk - 6-12 month timeline)

The mathematics indicate this system optimizes for selective resource extraction rather than universal care delivery. Under the Dominant Pattern Priority Rule, scattered instances of high-quality care at well-funded nonprofit facilities receive zero weight in scoring because selective benefit distribution is the extraction mechanism, not mitigation of it.

**Prognosis Without Intervention:**

Current trajectory projects accelerated fragmentation within 6-18 months as occupancy recovery exposes staffing infrastructure incapacity. The system cannot simultaneously meet growing demand, satisfy new admission requests (46-61 percent of facilities currently limiting admissions due to understaffing), and maintain minimal safety standards without fundamental architectural transformation. Observer withdrawal (families choosing any alternative to nursing home placement) is already measurable and accelerating.

## PHASE 1: STRUCTURAL DISSECTION - 7ES MAPPING

### Element 1: INPUT

**Primary Input Streams:**

The system consumes vulnerable human beings experiencing medical complexity that exceeds family caregiving capacity. These individuals enter through four primary pathways: post-acute hospital discharge requiring rehabilitation (Medicare-funded, 11-15 percent of resident days); chronic long-term care need after asset depletion (Medicaid-funded, 63 percent of residents); private-pay period pending Medicaid eligibility (24 percent of residents paying $8,000-$12,000 monthly); and Veterans Affairs contracted care (minor percentage).

Financial inputs consist of government payments constituting 59 percent of total industry revenue ($126.25 billion nationally in 2019), private payments from families during spend-down periods, and supplemental payment programs including Quality Incentive Payment Program ($1.75 billion annually in Texas alone).

Human capital inputs include registered nurses, licensed practical nurses, certified nursing assistants, and administrative personnel, with the industry experiencing 48.7 percent overall turnover, 63 percent CNA turnover, and 50 percent RN turnover as of 2024. Each RN replacement costs approximately $56,300, while CNA replacements cost $3,000-$6,000, creating annual turnover costs approximating $594,000 for a typical 100-bed facility.

**Subsystem Identification:**

The Input element contains at least three distinct subsystems operating through different processing mechanisms: Medicare admissions (processed through hospital discharge planning with 72+ hour delays common for Medicare Advantage prior authorization); Medicaid admissions (processed through state eligibility determination with asset verification requirements); and private-pay admissions (processed through financial qualification screening and market rate negotiation). Each subsystem has segregated processing pathways and distinct profit margins.

The labor input subsystem operates through fragmented hiring processes across multiple staffing agencies, direct recruitment, and temporary placements, with each pathway having different cost structures and quality implications.

**7ES INPUT ANALYSIS:**

This element exhibits extreme vulnerability through dependency on government payment systems that reimburse below cost for majority payer (Medicaid) while simultaneously depending on those payments for revenue stability. The dual-revenue structure (low-margin Medicaid/high-margin Medicare) creates architectural pressure to maximize Medicare census at expense of Medicaid residents, though Medicaid residents constitute the majority. The labor input crisis (occupancy recovered to 84 percent by October 2024 while 46-61 percent of facilities limit admissions due to staffing shortage) reveals that the system cannot process available human inputs even when demand exists and facilities have physical capacity.

The Input element demonstrates intentional design for selective processing: Medicare patients (higher acuity, higher payment, shorter stays) receive priority while Medicaid patients (lower payment, longer stays, similar or higher complexity) face systematic deprioritization through staffing allocation decisions. This is not resource scarcity; this is resource allocation architecture designed to extract maximum revenue from payment systems while externalizing actual care costs onto the most vulnerable input population.

### Element 2: OUTPUT

**Primary Output Categories:**

The stated output is provision of skilled nursing care, rehabilitation services, activities of daily living assistance, and safe residential environment for medically complex individuals. Measured outputs include: days of care provided (400 million nationally in 2019, mean 34,108 per facility); patient outcomes (hospital readmissions, emergency department visits, mortality, pressure ulcer development, medication errors, falls); Medicare and Medicaid billing revenues ($126.25 billion nationally in 2019, mean $10.75 million per facility); and regulatory compliance measures (inspection violations, deficiency citations, enforcement actions).

Actual outputs documented in Texas include: 143 preventable deaths since 2015 (120 in for-profit facilities); systematic understaffing with more than 50 percent of Texas facilities receiving one-star total staffing ratings; 58 percent receiving one-star registered nurse staffing ratings (second worst nationally); documented cases of residents dying with maggots in pressure sores, multiple rib fractures from falls without timely medical assessment, and violent patient-on-patient harm after repeated family warnings ignored.

Financial outputs include profit margins ranging from losses up to 161 percent to gains up to 83 percent (after winsorization), with industry reporting $730 million aggregate profit nationally (0.58 percent margin) but 8.84 percent margin when disallowed costs and depreciation excluded. Private equity-owned facilities generate systematically worse clinical outputs: 10 percent increased excess mortality, 50 percent increased antipsychotic prescriptions, 3 percent decreased frontline nursing hours, 11.1 percent increased preventable emergency department visits, 8.7 percent increased preventable hospitalizations, and 25 percent increased hospital-acquired conditions compared to non-PE for-profit facilities.

**Subsystem Identification:**

The Output element contains at least three functionally independent subsystems: clinical care delivery outputs (measured through patient outcomes, incident reports, regulatory violations); financial performance outputs (measured through billing, profit margins, return on investment); and regulatory compliance outputs (measured through inspection results, deficiency citations, enforcement actions, legal settlements). These subsystems target distinct recipient populations and optimize for different objectives.

Clinical outputs flow to residents and families as experienced quality of life and health status. Financial outputs flow to owners, investors, and parent companies as extractable profit. Regulatory compliance outputs flow to government oversight agencies as documented evidence of minimum standard adherence.

**7ES OUTPUT ANALYSIS:**

The Output element reveals the system's true optimization function. When clinical outcomes (preventable deaths, pressure ulcers, falls, infections) and financial outcomes (8.84 percent margins excluding disallowed costs, PE investment returns) are analyzed together, the pattern becomes mathematically clear: the system optimizes for financial extraction rather than clinical quality. This is not inefficiency; this is precision engineering.

The bifurcation between stated outputs (safe, skilled nursing care) and actual outputs (preventable deaths alongside substantial profit margins) demonstrates what the DQD framework identifies as Goal Alignment failure. The system reliably produces financial returns for owners while simultaneously producing preventable harm for residents. This dual output pattern is structurally maintained through litigation immunity ($250,000 caps making cases economically infeasible), weak enforcement (only 3 state fines in 3 years, average $15,549 penalty per death), and regulatory capture (successful April 2025 lawsuit eliminating federal staffing minimums).

The private equity subsystem represents Output optimization in pure form: 10 percent increased mortality is not a bug, it is the mathematical result of extracting 3 percent more nursing hours while maintaining revenue. When PE firms acquire facilities, they do not mysteriously become worse at care delivery; they systematically reallocate resources from clinical operations (labor) to financial returns (investor distributions), and the clinical outputs (excess deaths, preventable harms) are the designed consequence of that resource reallocation.

### Element 3: PROCESSING

**Core Processing Mechanisms:**

The transformation of vulnerable human inputs into care outputs theoretically occurs through: clinical assessment and care planning; medication management and administration; assistance with activities of daily living (feeding, bathing, toileting, mobility); wound care and medical monitoring; rehabilitation services (physical, occupational, speech therapy); social services and discharge planning; and 24-hour nursing supervision.

Actual processing as documented reveals: systematic understaffing creating situations where single nurses supervise 165 residents (documented Iowa case resulting in death, but Texas ratios comparable); medication administration errors occurring with sufficient frequency to appear in multiple facility violation reports; pressure ulcer development indicating inadequate turning and repositioning protocols; falls without timely medical assessment indicating inadequate monitoring; and delayed emergency response documented across multiple facilities.

Financial processing mechanisms include: Medicare billing optimization through acuity coding strategies; related-party transactions ($11 billion annually nationwide, 9.54 percent of revenues) structured to extract resources from operating entities to owner-controlled service providers; depreciation and disallowed cost strategies ($6.42 billion in disallowed costs nationally that never require repayment); and complex ownership structures (facility-level LLCs separated from final ownership through additional LLC layers) designed to obscure accountability and facilitate resource extraction.

**Subsystem Identification:**

The Processing element contains at least four distinct transformation subsystems operating at different scales and frequencies: direct clinical care processing (continuous, 24-hour cycle, performed by frontline nursing staff); financial processing (daily billing cycles, monthly revenue recognition, quarterly profit distribution); regulatory processing (periodic inspection preparation, deficiency response, documentation management); and ownership structure processing (annual financial reporting, periodic restructuring, merger/acquisition activity).

Each subsystem has distinct input types handled, operational scales, and temporal frequencies. Clinical processing operates continuously with immediate resident impact. Financial processing operates on billing cycles with deferred revenue recognition. Regulatory processing operates reactively with multi-month lag times. Ownership processing operates strategically with multi-year time horizons.

**7ES PROCESSING ANALYSIS:**

The Processing element exposes the system's extraction architecture most clearly. Clinical care processing and financial profit processing compete for the same constrained input (labor), creating a zero-sum relationship: every dollar extracted as profit is a dollar unavailable for nurse wages, and every nursing hour cut is additional profit for extraction.

The documented processing outcomes reveal architectural prioritization: financial processing mechanisms are sophisticated, comprehensive, and optimized (complex LLC structures, related-party transaction management, depreciation strategies, disallowed cost maximization), while clinical processing mechanisms are deliberately under-resourced (one nurse for 165 residents, medication error rates, pressure ulcer development, falls without assessment).

This is not resource scarcity. Texas is the eighth largest economy in the world. This is designed resource allocation: the same facilities that cannot afford adequate nursing staff somehow generate profit margins up to 83 percent and successfully fund multi-million-dollar lobbying campaigns to overturn federal staffing standards.

The regulatory processing subsystem functions as extraction enabler rather than quality enforcement. Multi-month lag times between violations and enforcement, minimal financial penalties ($15,549 average per death), and successful industry litigation blocking staffing standards create a regulatory processing framework that facilitates extraction rather than constraining it.

The private equity processing model represents the extraction mechanism in pure algorithmic form: acquire facility, reduce labor costs (the largest expense category at two-thirds of revenue), maintain or increase billing through acuity optimization, extract profit, repeat. The resulting clinical outputs (10 percent excess mortality, 50 percent increased antipsychotic use, 25 percent increased hospital-acquired conditions) are not unintended consequences; they are the mathematical result of the processing optimization function.

### Element 4: CONTROLS

**Formal Control Mechanisms:**

Federal controls include Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services certification requirements, Medicare Conditions of Participation, Medicaid program standards, Nursing Home Reform Law requirements, and the recently vacated minimum staffing standards (requiring 3.48 hours per resident day including 0.55 hours RN time and 2.45 hours nurse aide time, overturned April 2025 by Texas federal court).

State controls include Texas Health and Human Services Commission Long-Term Care Regulatory (HHSC LTCR) division oversight, facility licensing requirements (Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 242, 26 TAC Chapter 554), annual inspections, complaint investigations, and enforcement authority including fines and license revocation. The state conducts inspections and investigations with documented authority to enforce actions against facilities found in violation.

Financial controls include Medicare cost report requirements, Medicaid rate-setting methodologies, Quality Incentive Payment Program participation requirements ($1.75 billion annually in Texas), and Direct Care Staff Enhancement Program voluntary participation. Texas implemented Patient Driven Payment Model for Long-Term Care (PDPM LTC) effective September 1, 2025, as the reimbursement methodology.

Legal controls include tort liability limits ($250,000 cap on noneconomic damages per defendant, $750,000 maximum across all defendants, enacted 2003), expert report requirements for malpractice claims, and statutes of limitations. Civil monetary penalties for violations range from $14,807 average per-instance to $48,327 average per-day, with some cases exceeding $200,000.

**Actual Control Function:**

The formal control structure creates appearance of constraint while systematically enabling extraction. The April 2025 Texas federal court decision overturning minimum staffing standards represents control system collapse: industry successfully litigated away the primary constraint that would have prevented continued extraction through understaffing.

Enforcement data reveals control system impotence: only 3 state-imposed fines in Texas over three years despite 143 documented preventable deaths since 2015; average penalty of $15,549 per death (compare to facility average revenue of $10.75 million annually); and successful industry resistance to enhanced federal oversight through coordinated multi-state litigation.

The tort liability cap functions as extraction enabler rather than care constraint. By making most negligence cases economically infeasible to pursue (attorneys report cases cost $50,000-$100,000 to develop, while cap limits recovery), the control removes the primary private enforcement mechanism that would otherwise constrain harmful conduct. Texas ranks 49th nationally in malpractice payouts per capita ($3.20), and resolved claims dropped by two-thirds following 2003 reform, with average payouts falling 22 percent to $199,000.

**Subsystem Identification:**

The Controls element contains at least three distinct constraint subsystems operating at different hierarchical layers and temporal frequencies: federal regulatory controls (Medicare/Medicaid program standards, CMS oversight, multi-year enforcement cycles); state regulatory controls (HHSC inspection, complaint investigation, annual cycles); and legal liability controls (tort system, civil monetary penalties, case-by-case basis). Each constraint type operates through different enforcement mechanisms and temporal scales.

**7ES CONTROLS ANALYSIS:**

The Controls element reveals the most damning evidence of intentional design for extraction rather than care. Every control that would meaningfully constrain profit extraction at expense of resident safety has been systematically neutralized through lobbying, litigation, or legislative action.

Consider the control architecture sequence:

1. **Federal staffing standards proposed** (would require adequate nursing ratios)  
   **Industry response:** Multi-jurisdiction litigation, coordinated lobbying  
   **Outcome:** Standards overturned April 2025 by Texas federal judge

2. **State enforcement authority exists** (fines, license revocation)  
   **Industry response:** Not needed - enforcement rarely exercised  
   **Outcome:** Only 3 fines in 3 years despite 143 preventable deaths

3. **Private litigation could constrain** (wrongful death, negligence claims)  
   **Industry response:** Lobbied for and achieved $250,000 damages cap in 2003  
   **Outcome:** Most cases economically infeasible, claims down two-thirds

4. **Transparency could enable oversight** (ownership disclosure, financial reporting)  
   **Industry response:** Complex LLC structures, related-party transactions  
   **Outcome:** Until 2023 CMS rule, ownership largely opaque

This is not regulatory failure. This is regulatory capture operating exactly as designed. The controls exist to create legitimacy appearance while systematically failing to constrain extraction.

The most revealing control characteristic: controls on financial extraction (Medicare disallowed cost repayment requirements, state Medicaid rate audits, related-party transaction limits) are systematically weaker than controls on resident protection (which are themselves weak). Medicare does not require facilities to return disallowed costs ($6.42 billion nationally), meaning the financial control system actually subsidizes extraction through overpayment without recapture.

### Element 6: INTERFACE

**Critical System Boundaries:**

The primary interfaces where this system mediates exchange with incompatible external regimes include:

**Hospital-to-Nursing Home Interface:** This boundary manages transitions from acute care to post-acute rehabilitation or long-term care. The interface requires medical information transfer, care plan translation, equipment and medication coordination, and financial authorization (particularly for Medicare Advantage requiring 72+ hour prior authorization creating discharge delays). Compatibility standards enforced include medical necessity criteria, financial eligibility verification, and bed availability matching. The interface frequently fails through inadequate discharge planning, incomplete information transfer, inappropriate patient-facility matching, and financial authorization delays.

**Family-to-Facility Interface:** This boundary manages care decision delegation, financial arrangement, information access, and advocacy attempts. The interface is structured through admissions contracts (often containing mandatory arbitration clauses), visitor policies, complaint procedures, and power of attorney or guardianship arrangements. Compatibility enforcement includes financial qualification screening, legal contract execution, and family member background checks. The interface systematically fails family members through information asymmetry (families cannot assess quality before placement), financial pressure (crisis placements during hospital discharge), contractual power imbalance, and complaint system ineffectiveness.

**Regulatory-to-Operations Interface:** This boundary manages inspection scheduling, deficiency citation, correction verification, and enforcement determination. The interface requires documentation production, facility access, staff interviews, and correction plan submission. The multi-month lag between violations and enforcement, limited inspection frequency (annual standard inspections), and complaint-driven rather than proactive monitoring create interface gaps where ongoing harms persist without regulatory detection or intervention.

**Labor Market Interface:** This boundary manages nursing recruitment, training, retention, and workforce development. The interface must translate market wage rates into facility compensation decisions, but systematic failure occurs when facilities refuse to pay market-clearing wages due to profit margin protection. The resulting 48.7 percent turnover, 63 percent CNA turnover, and 50 percent RN turnover indicate that the interface is rejecting available labor inputs due to financial constraints that are chosen rather than necessary.

**Subsystem Identification:**

The Interface element contains at least four distinct transduction subsystems operating through different compatibility mechanisms: medical information transduction (hospital to facility, requiring standardized assessment formats and clinical terminology translation); financial authorization transduction (payer to facility, requiring insurance verification, prior authorization, and payment processing); regulatory compliance transduction (government standards to operational practices, requiring documentation systems and reporting mechanisms); and family communication transduction (lay understanding to medical complexity, requiring interpretation and simplification).

**7ES INTERFACE ANALYSIS:**

The Interface element reveals how extraction architecture is protected through incompatibility. Each interface is designed to prevent rather than facilitate the information flows and resource transfers that would enable accountability or constrain harmful practices.

### FDP 8: INTELLECTUAL HONESTY (Weight: 2)

**Formula:** IH = 10 × (1 - Hidden trade-offs / Total trade-offs)

**Trade-Off Analysis:**

**Acknowledged Trade-Offs:**

The nursing home industry openly acknowledges limited trade-offs:

1. **Cost-Quality Trade-Off (Partially Acknowledged):** Industry acknowledges that higher staffing levels would improve quality but claims this requires unsustainable cost increases or rate increases that government will not fund. This is partial acknowledgment: industry admits staffing affects quality but frames resource constraint as external (government underpayment) rather than internal (profit extraction choices).

2. **Safety-Autonomy Trade-Off (Acknowledged):** Industry acknowledges that safety interventions (monitoring, restrictions, supervision) reduce resident autonomy and discusses need to balance fall prevention against mobility preservation. This is one of few genuinely acknowledged trade-offs.

3. **Specialization-Scale Trade-Off (Partially Acknowledged):** Some industry representatives acknowledge that large chain operations may sacrifice individualized care for operational efficiency. However, this is framed as business model variation rather than systematic quality trade-off.

**Hidden or Denied Trade-Offs:**

The following critical trade-offs are systematically hidden, denied, or misrepresented:

1. **Profit-Safety Trade-Off (Hidden):** The fundamental trade-off between investor/owner profit extraction and resident safety is systematically denied. Industry claims profit and quality are aligned ("well-run facilities are profitable; poorly-run facilities lose money"). However, documented evidence demonstrates inverse relationship: PE ownership produces 10% excess mortality while maintaining profitability through labor cost reduction. This trade-off is not acknowledged; instead, industry frames adequacy standards as "unfunded mandates" rather than as profit protection elimination.

*Evidence of Hiding:* Industry fierce resistance to minimum staffing standards (successful litigation to overturn federal requirements) reveals that acknowledging adequate staffing would require acknowledging current profit margins are extracted from inadequate care. Standing ovation at industry meeting upon learning standards were overturned demonstrates collective acknowledgment that profitability depends on ability to understaff, but this is never stated publicly.

2. **Payer Mix-Care Quality Trade-Off (Hidden):** Facilities systematically prioritize profitable Medicare patients over unprofitable Medicaid patients but deny that this creates care quality stratification. The systematic difference in outcomes between payer types (documented in research) is not acknowledged by industry as intentional resource allocation choice.

*Evidence of Hiding:* Medicare patients receive $587/day vs Medicaid $270/day, yet industry claims equivalent care quality. However, 120 of 143 preventable deaths in Texas since 2015 occurred in for-profit facilities serving predominantly Medicaid populations. This payer-based outcome difference is not acknowledged as intentional resource allocation trade-off; instead, it is framed as unfortunate correlation or blamed on patient characteristics.

3. **Ownership Complexity-Accountability Trade-Off (Hidden):** Complex LLC structures, related-party transactions, and REIT separations obscure accountability but industry claims these structures are necessary for "operational efficiency" and "capital formation" rather than acknowledging they also serve to diffuse liability and prevent oversight.

*Evidence of Hiding:* Until forced by 2023 CMS rule, industry resisted ownership disclosure requirements that would make complex structures transparent. If structures served only legitimate efficiency purposes, transparency would be welcomed rather than resisted. The resistance reveals that opacity serves extraction protection function that industry will not acknowledge.

4. **Litigation Immunity-Accountability Trade-Off (Hidden):** The 2003 tort reform created $250,000 damages cap that effectively eliminates legal accountability for most negligence causing death or injury. Industry framed this as necessary to reduce "frivolous lawsuits" and control insurance costs, not acknowledging that it also eliminates genuine accountability for preventable harms.

*Evidence of Hiding:* Industry claims tort reform "moved nonmeritorious, marginal cases out of the system" but does not acknowledge that it also prevented legitimate cases from proceeding. The claim that cases dropped two-thirds because they were frivolous (rather than because they became economically infeasible to pursue) is intellectual dishonesty. Attorneys report that $50,000-$100,000 case development costs make $250,000 recovery ceiling insufficient to pursue most legitimate cases, but industry does not acknowledge this as accountability elimination trade-off.

This fundamental trade-off between investor returns and resident welfare is completely denied by PE industry.

*Evidence of Hiding:* PE firms claim they "professionalize management" and "bring operational excellence" rather than acknowledging they extract returns through labor cost reduction that degrades care quality. The systematic worse outcomes documented in research are not acknowledged by PE firms; instead, they frame acquisitions as "turnaround investments" that will improve facilities over time. Evidence shows the opposite: outcomes worsen post-acquisition and remain worse throughout PE ownership.

This trade-off is not acknowledged; instead, industry frames lobbying positions as protecting "access to care" (claiming facilities will close if required to meet adequacy standards) rather than protecting extraction operations.

*Evidence of Hiding:* Industry claims fighting staffing standards protects facilities from closure (access preservation frame) but does not acknowledge this means protecting current profit margins that depend on understaffing. The fundamental trade-off is not between standards and access; it is between standards and profitability. Industry will not acknowledge this.

The choice to prioritize capital returns and executive compensation over frontline wages is not acknowledged as trade-off; instead, worker compensation inadequacy is framed as external constraint (reimbursement inadequacy) rather than internal allocation choice.

8. **Quality Improvement Coordination:** Requires coordination between: data collection, analysis, improvement planning, implementation, monitoring, sustained adherence. Coordination failures lead to quality decline.
