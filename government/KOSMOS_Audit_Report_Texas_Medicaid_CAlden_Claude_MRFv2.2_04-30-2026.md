## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Texas Medicaid operates as a deliberately architected exclusionary system that concentrates healthcare access among politically defensible populations (children, pregnant women, elderly, disabled) while systematically excluding 617,000 to 920,000 working-age adults through income thresholds calibrated to guarantee failure. The system exhibits a dominant extractive pattern where operational architecture cannot achieve stated public health objectives even under optimal conditions.

**Critical Metrics:**
- Coverage Gap Population: 617,000-920,000 adults (42% of national total)
- State Uninsured Rate: 16% (2× national average, highest in U.S.)
- Parent Income Eligibility: 15% FPL ($234/month for family of 3)
- Adults Without Children: 0% eligible regardless of income
- Maternal Mortality: 29.3 per 100,000 births (D grade)
- Infant Mortality: 5.8 per 1,000 (1.7× higher for Black infants)

**Dominant Pattern Classification:** Extractive  
**System Architecture:** Privilege-Calibrated Exclusion  
**Global FDP Score:** 1.8 (Unnatural, Collapse-Prone)  
**Observer Collapse Function (OCF):** 0.89 (Maximum Collapse Risk)  
**Predicted Timeline:** 2-4 years without federal intervention

The Dominant Pattern Priority Rule mandates scoring based on the extractive burden experience rather than averaging across participants. This audit reflects the operational reality for the 617,000+ in the coverage gap and the 3.2 million total uninsured Texans, not the managed care experience of the 4.2 million enrolled.

## PHASE 1: STRUCTURAL DISSECTION (7ES FRAMEWORK)

### Element 1: Input (Resources Entering System)

**Identified Inputs:**
1. **Federal Funds:** ~60% FMAP (Federal Medical Assistance Percentage) for base program; 90% match available for expansion (rejected)
2. **State Appropriations:** Texas General Revenue allocated to HHSC
3. **Managed Care Premiums:** Capitated payments to MCOs
4. **Eligible Population:** 4.2M enrolled (primarily children, elderly, disabled)
5. **Excluded Population:** 617,000-920,000 in coverage gap; 3.2M+ total uninsured

**Subsystem Analysis:**
The Input architecture exhibits three distinct subsystems:
- **Financial Input (Federal/State):** Rejected $10 billion annually in federal expansion funds
- **Population Sorting Mechanism:** Income thresholds calibrated at 15% FPL for parents (among lowest nationally)
- **Managed Care Contracting:** Private MCO selection through RFP process

**7ES Input Weaknesses:**
The system deliberately restricts eligible input population through privilege-calibrated barriers. Parent income threshold of $234/month for family of three represents 15% of Federal Poverty Level—a threshold explicitly designed to exclude the working poor while maintaining political defensibility through coverage of children.

Adult exclusion creates a two-tiered input architecture where medical need is subordinated to categorical eligibility. A 35-year-old working adult earning $800/month faces identical exclusion as one earning $80,000/month—the system makes no distinction because both fall outside designed categories.

### Element 2: Output (Results System Produces)

**Identified Outputs:**
1. **Healthcare Services:** 4.2M enrolled receive managed care benefits
2. **Systematic Exclusion:** 617,000-920,000 denied coverage despite poverty
3. **Health Outcomes:** Maternal mortality 29.3/100K (D grade), infant mortality 5.8/1K
4. **Economic Burden Transfer:** Uncompensated care costs shifted to counties, hospitals
5. **Racial Disparities:** Black infant mortality 1.7× average; Black preterm birth 15% vs 10% white

**Output Quality Assessment:**
Primary Output is not healthcare delivery but population filtration. For every person receiving coverage, 0.77 people in poverty are systematically excluded. The system produces health outcomes inferior to expansion states while claiming administrative efficiency.

Managed care organizations report 87-90% of revenue spent on direct care with 10-14% administrative overhead. However, this metric excludes the administrative complexity imposed on excluded populations who must navigate:
- Emergency-only care fragmentation
- County indigent programs with varying eligibility
- Federally Qualified Health Centers at capacity
- Charity care applications with inconsistent standards

**7ES Output Weaknesses:**
The Output element functions as designed but toward extractive rather than regenerative ends. MCO efficiency metrics measure optimization within the privileged subset, not system-wide health outcome production. A managed care plan achieving 90% cost effectiveness for enrolled members while 20% of eligible populations remain uninsured has not achieved efficiency—it has achieved precision in exclusion.

### Element 3: Processing (Transformation Mechanisms)

**Identified Processing Subsystems:**

**A. Eligibility Determination Processing:**
- **MAGI Methodology:** Modified Adjusted Gross Income calculations for most categories
- **SSI Auto-Enrollment:** Supplemental Security Income recipients enrolled automatically
- **Categorical Filtering:** Adults without children categorically excluded
- **Income Verification:** Real-time data matching through federal hub
- **Processing Timeline:** 45-90 days for disability determinations

**B. Managed Care Processing:**
- **MCO Assignment:** Geographic and program-based allocation to 15+ contracted MCOs
- **Capitated Payment:** Fixed per-member-per-month rates to MCOs
- **Service Coordination:** Care management for STAR+PLUS, STAR Kids populations
- **Prior Authorization:** MCO-specific approval processes
- **Claims Adjudication:** Private MCO systems

**C. Administrative Processing:**
- **Federal Reporting:** CMS compliance and encounter data submission
- **Auditing:** State Auditor oversight; MCO financial reviews
- **Quality Measurement:** HEDIS, CAHPS metrics; performance improvement projects
- **Fraud Detection:** Program integrity investigations

**Processing Complexity Assessment:**
The Processing architecture exhibits deliberate complexity layering. While managed care streamlines service delivery for enrolled populations, the categorical eligibility structure creates insurmountable barriers for excluded groups.

Complexity serves dual functions:
1. **For Privileged Populations:** Automated enrollment (SSI), simplified renewals (children), extended postpartum coverage (12 months)
2. **For Excluded Populations:** Categorical impossibility regardless of administrative competence

A parent earning $235/month for a family of three cannot process their way into eligibility—no amount of documentation, medical need demonstration, or case management overcomes the categorical exclusion. Processing efficiency within the system is irrelevant when Processing access is calibrated by privilege.

**7ES Processing Weaknesses:**
Processing demonstrates state capacity for sophisticated eligibility automation and managed care coordination. This capacity's selective deployment reveals design intent: the State possesses technical infrastructure to process expansion populations but chooses architectural exclusion over technical limitation.

The Processing element scores low not due to incompetence but due to deliberate miscalibration.

**Dominant Pattern Application:**
Per Dominant Pattern Priority Rule, system exhibits extractive pattern where operational architecture cannot achieve stated purpose (healthcare for low-income Texans) for all eligible participants. Scoring must reflect experience of extractive burden population.

For the 617,000 in coverage gap and 3.2M uninsured:
- Benefits received: Emergency-only care; fragmented safety net
- Costs imposed: Medical debt; preventable death; delayed care

**Calculation:**
Benefits to all stakeholders = 0.1 (emergency care access only for excluded majority)
Benefits to controllers = 1.0 (enrolled receive full coverage; MCOs profit; political objectives achieved)

SP = 10 × (0.1 / 1.0) = 1.0

**Final Score: SP = 1.0**
